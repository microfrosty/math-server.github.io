The number $0$ seems obvious now. It was anything but.

## The Problem with Nothing

Greek mathematicians, who gave us Euclidean geometry and rigorous proof, had no symbol for zero. Numbers, to Aristotle, described quantities of things. The concept of *nothing* as a quantity was philosophically troubled — you cannot have a collection of zero apples in the same sense you have three.

This wasn't ignorance. It was a philosophical commitment: mathematics should describe the world, and the world contains no empty quantities.

## The Babylonian Placeholder

The Babylonians used a positional number system (base 60, still with us in hours, minutes, seconds) and eventually developed a **placeholder symbol** to distinguish, say, $1\,2$ from $1\,0\,2$. But this was punctuation, not a number. You couldn't compute with it.

## Brahmagupta's Rules (628 CE)

The Indian mathematician **Brahmagupta** was the first to treat zero as a number and state rules for computing with it:

- $a + 0 = a$
- $a - 0 = a$  
- $a \times 0 = 0$
- $0 / 0 = 0$ ← *he got this one wrong*

His treatment of $0/0 = 0$ and $a/0$ was incorrect by modern standards, but defining zero as a number you can add, subtract, and multiply was revolutionary.

## The Transmission to Europe

Zero traveled from India to the Islamic world, where al-Khwarizmi incorporated it into his treatise on algebra (from which we get the word *algorithm* — a Latinization of his name). From there it reached Europe, where it met fierce resistance.

In 1299, Florence **banned the use of the Hindu-Arabic numerals** (including zero) in commercial transactions, preferring Roman numerals. The fear was that zero and the flexible positional notation made fraud too easy — numbers were too easily altered.

## Zero in Mathematics

Once accepted, zero unleashed mathematics. Calculus depends on the concept of a limit approaching zero. The integer ring $\Z$ requires an additive identity. The **Peano axioms** begin with $0$.

More subtly, zero allows us to define **algebraic structures** cleanly. A field requires an additive identity $0$ and multiplicative identity $1$ with $0 \neq 1$. Linear algebra requires the zero vector. Category theory has the zero morphism.

The empty set $\emptyset$ is zero's set-theoretic counterpart — the foundation of the von Neumann construction of the natural numbers:

$$0 = \emptyset, \quad 1 = \{\emptyset\}, \quad 2 = \{\emptyset, \{\emptyset\}\}, \quad \ldots$$

## Nothing Is Complicated

Division by zero remains undefined (or $\pm\infty$ in the extended reals, or absorbed into projective geometry). IEEE 754 floating-point arithmetic distinguishes $+0$ from $-0$. In the $p$-adic numbers, zero is surrounded by an entire metric structure unlike anything in ordinary analysis.

Zero is the simplest number. It is also, in some ways, the most profound.
