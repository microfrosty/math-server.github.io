Basic Mathematics by Lang claims to provide a firm foundation for a course in Calculus. The book is split into five parts: Algebra, Intuitive Geometry, Coordinate Geometry, and Miscellaneous.
 
## Algebra
 
The Algebra portion has the reader spending most of their time proving basic properties of numbers (commutativity of addition, etc.) and very briefly covers solving linear equations with two or three unknowns and quadratic equations. Within this first part, the major issue with the book is revealed. Lang does not spend enough time on extremely relevant topics for Calculus and instead tends to spend the majority of the text proving basic properties of numbers which the reader is probably already familiar with. In addition, for someone not familiar with the material, I do not think it would be a good first experience. It is simply too brief.
 
## Intuitive Geometry
 
The next part is Intuitive Geometry, which surprisingly is where the book peaks. It is extremely well written, with the only con being that it is not rigorous in certain places, which may cause frustration for some readers.
 
## Coordinate Geometry
 
The next part is Coordinate Geometry, whose main purpose should be to introduce the trigonometric functions. However, Lang spends the majority of the time discussing distances between points, relating it back to the part on intuitive geometry. A large number of students struggle in Calculus because they are not familiar enough with the details of the trigonometric functions, and in this regard the book is disappointing.
 
## Miscellaneous
 
The final part, titled Miscellaneous, covers functions, mappings, complex numbers, induction and summations, and determinants. This part is a little all over the place, with some chapters - such as determinants - being far too brief to be useful, and other chapters - such as mappings, which has an amazing section on permutations - being very interesting with nice questions. It is also a big positive that Lang introduces induction.
 
## Verdict
 
Overall, the book varies chapter by chapter and may not be worth it for those with no experience in these topics or for those with experience in analysis already. However, for those between those skill levels, there is certainly value in working through the book, if only for the intuitive geometry portion.
